Plain Language & Bureaucracy Simplification

What is Plain Language ‚Äì and when is it useful?

Plain Language is a highly simplified form of language with fixed rules. It uses short sentences (max. 8-12 words), simple grammar (active instead of passive), clear words without jargon and logical structure.

Target audience: People with learning difficulties, cognitive limitations, low reading skills or language learners.

Legally required for: Federal authorities (Germany), public websites (BITV 2.0) and election information.

Characteristics:

  • Short sentences (max. 8-12 words)
  • Familiar words, no jargon without explanation
  • Active instead of passive
  • Concrete time information instead of 'reasonable period'
  • Visual aids and line breaks after sense units

Example:

Before: "Within the framework of residence permit examination, the submission of biometric photographs is mandatory."

After: "You need new photos for your application. The photos must look like this: biometric. Bring 2 photos with you."

What is the difference between Plain Language and Easy-to-Read?

Plain Language follows strict rules, Easy-to-Read is more flexible.

Plain Language:
Max. 8-12 words per sentence, no genitive, no subjunctive, test group recommended
Easy-to-Read:
Max. 15-20 words, subordinate clauses allowed, genitive sparingly, no mandatory test group

Core differences:

  • Plain Language = maximally accessible, strictly regulated
  • Easy-to-Read = generally understandable, pragmatic
  • Plain Language: 8-12 words/sentence, Easy-to-Read: 15-20 words/sentence
  • Plain Language often needs test group, Easy-to-Read doesn't
  • Both improve accessibility, but different rigor

Plain Language target: People with cognitive limitations

Easy-to-Read target: General population, including functional illiterates

When is Plain Language legally required?

Legal requirement for: Federal authorities (notices, forms, administrative procedures in Germany), public institutions (websites, apps according to BITV 2.0), election information and contracts with disability workshops.

Recommended for: NGOs, foundations (donation appeals), healthcare (patient information), educational institutions and companies (customer communication).

Plain Language is worthwhile for broader accessibility and better customer experience even without legal obligation.

  • Requirement: Federal authorities, public websites, elections
  • Recommended: NGOs, healthcare, education, customer service
  • Compliance risk decreases with adherence
  • Improves accessibility even without legal obligation
How do I check Plain Language quality without a test group?

Test groups are ideal but not always available. Alternative quality assurance:

  4. Read text aloud (stumbling points become apparent)

  6. Pilot test with small user group

What are the most common mistakes in Plain Language?

Top 5 mistakes:

  1. Sentences too long ‚Äì more than 12 words
  2. Passive constructions instead of active ('It is checked' ‚Üí 'We check')
  3. Foreign words without explanation ('biometric' ‚Üí 'biometric (that means: photo according to fixed rules)')
  4. Genitive instead of simple construction ('The examination of the application' ‚Üí 'The office examines your application')
  5. Metaphors and idioms ('put application on the way' ‚Üí 'submit application')

Example Before: "The office checks whether you meet the requirements and whether all documents are complete."

Example After: "The office checks your documents. The office checks: Are all papers there?"

LLM-Readiness & Machine Readability

How do I make texts LLM-friendly? (7 criteria)

7 criteria for machine- and human-readable texts:

  1. Clear heading as question ('What do you need to do?' instead of 'Required measures')
  2. Bullet points instead of continuous text
  3. Concrete deadlines ('by November 30' instead of 'within reasonable period')
  4. Active instead of passive
  5. No double negatives
  6. Contact info visible (email, phone directly in text)
  7. Give examples ('e.g. ID card' instead of just 'required documents')

Why LLM-friendly? Chatbots extract structured information precisely.

  • Formulate headings as questions
  • Bullet points instead of continuous text
  • Name concrete dates/deadlines
  • Use active instead of passive
  • Avoid double negatives
  • Place contact directly in text
  • Examples instead of abstract terms

How do I make our tender LLM-friendly? (5 steps)

5 steps for LLM-friendly tenders:

  1. Check structure ‚Äì Headings as questions ('What is sought?', 'What requirements?', 'How to apply?') with H2/H3
  2. Core info extractable ‚Äì Deadline, budget, requirements as bullet points or table
  3. Terms consistent ‚Äì always 'applicant' OR 'interested party', don't alternate
  4. Spell out abbreviations ‚Äì at first occurrence 'European Union (EU)'
  5. Give examples ‚Äì 'Reference project = completed project in similar area, max. 3 years old'

Effect: ChatGPT, Copilot can precisely summarize tender.
